## Hypothesis
If NS iterate blending works because it combines two "views" of the gradient at different processing stages, the principle should generalize. Test: two momentum buffers at different time constants (β=0.80 for curvature/recent, β=0.95 for direction/stable), blended before or after NS.

## Architecture
```
grad → [fast momentum (β=0.80)] → ┐
       [slow momentum (β=0.95)] → ┤→ blend → NS → update  (pre_ns)
                                   └→ NS each → blend → update  (post_ns)
```

## Key Formula
- **Pre-NS:** `u = (1-b)*nesterov(slow) + b*scale(nesterov(fast))`  then NS(u)
- **Post-NS:** `(1-b)*NS(nesterov(slow)) + b*scale(NS(nesterov(fast)))`

## Cost
- Pre-NS: One extra momentum buffer (same NS cost as Muon)
- Post-NS: Two NS passes (2x NS cost) + extra momentum buffer

## Analysis

### Input-blend is a breakthrough (8v1 input_blend mode)
- **-0.011 vs Muon at matrix-path speed** (48s vs 213s for 5v6)
- Fastest early convergence of ANY optimizer tested: 2.339 at step 250, 1.759 at step 500
- Matches 5v6 quality from step 1500 onward
- EMA of past NS₅ outputs cancels oscillation across TIME (different steps), just like v5 cancels across ITERATIONS (different NS steps within one iteration)
- 4.4x faster than 5v6 for ~0.002 quality gap

### Dual-momentum pre_ns FAILS (+0.0045 vs Muon)
- Confirms learning #14: NS washes out input modifications
- Two momentum signals that differ by 15% produce NS outputs that differ by <1%
- NS's Jacobian is near-zero around its fixed point — kills input perturbations
- This proves the improvement from blending is about NS OUTPUT, not NS INPUT

## Verdict
**Input-blend is the new best practical approach.** Near-5v6 quality at Muon speed. Dual-momentum pre_ns fails as predicted. The principle "temporal averaging of optimizer subroutine outputs" is confirmed as novel and effective.
