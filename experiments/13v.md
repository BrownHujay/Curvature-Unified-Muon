parameterization that achieves near-perfect SV equalization:

- **Minimax-optimal** (min max |p⁵(σ) - 0.88|): a=2.6806, b=-3.6311, c=1.8871. Max error = 0.0004.
- **Min-variance** (min Var[p⁵(σ)]): a=2.2311, b=-3.2137, c=1.9518. Var ≈ 0. Mean = 0.779.
- **L2-optimal** (min MSE to 0.88): a=2.6704, b=-3.6166, c=1.8848. MSE ≈ 0.

These have NO fixed point at σ*=0.868 — they're outside the bifurcation family entirely.
For comparison, standard Muon has Var[p⁵] = 0.031, d=-1.0 has 0.002, these have ~0.000.

If near-perfect equalization without oscillation works, it changes the story entirely.
If it fails (like stable-0.88 did), it confirms that some oscillation IS needed even with blending.

## Analysis

### Why minimax-optimal polynomials fail despite "perfect" equalization

The core insight: **equalization quality (Var[p⁵]) is the wrong metric.** What matters is the SPEED of small-SV inflation.

**Coefficient magnitude determines SV inflation rate:**
- Standard NS: a=3.4445 → σ=0.1 inflates to ~0.34 per step
- d=-1.0 bifurcation: a=3.1512 → σ=0.1 inflates to ~0.32 per step
- Minimax optimal: a=2.6806 → σ=0.1 inflates to ~0.27 per step
- Minvar: a=2.2311 → σ=0.1 inflates to ~0.23 per step

The linear coefficient `a` directly controls first-step inflation of small SVs. Minimax has ~22% less first-step amplification than standard NS. Minvar has ~35% less. After 5 iterations, this compounds — small SVs simply don't get inflated fast enough to contribute meaningfully to the update.

**The bifurcation family constraint is NOT incidental:**
The bifurcation family parameterizes polynomials by p'(σ*) while preserving the fixed point at σ*=0.868. This FORCES large enough coefficients because a = (3-d)/2 + c·σ*⁴ where c=2.0315 contributes a base ~1.14, and d ∈ [-1, -3] pushes a into [2.0, 3.5]. The minimax-optimal polynomials, found by unconstrained optimization, have NO such structural guarantee — they achieve equalization by being "gentle" (small coefficients) rather than "aggressive then cancel" (large coefficients + blending).

**Hierarchy of what matters for NS polynomial performance:**
1. **Coefficient magnitude** (a ≥ ~3.0): fast small-SV inflation is non-negotiable
2. **Oscillation management** (blending): cancel the oscillation that large coefficients cause
3. **Equalization quality** (Var[p⁵]): least important — 17x worse equalization (d=-1.0 vs minimax) still wins

**Comparison to stable-0.88 failure (Series 11):**
Stable-0.88 had a=2.0 and failed catastrophically (+0.045 vs Muon). Minimax has a=2.68 and failed moderately (+0.017 vs combined). Minvar has a=2.23 and failed worse (+0.020 vs Muon). This forms a clear dose-response: lower `a` → worse performance, regardless of equalization quality.

**Why minimax combined barely beats Muon (-0.002) while basic is much worse:**
Combined mode's temporal EMA partially compensates for slow convergence by accumulating across steps. But it can't overcome the fundamental deficit — each step contributes less useful signal because small SVs aren't inflated enough.

**Why minimax TD is Muon-tier despite full recipe treatment:**
TD(λ) blending works by canceling oscillation across intermediate iterates. With minimax polynomial, there IS less oscillation to cancel (that's the whole point), but there's also less aggressive equalization to preserve. The recipe has nothing to work with.

## Verdict

**FAILED.** Coefficient magnitude matters more than equalization quality. The `a` coefficient must be ≥~3.0 for fast small-SV inflation. Minimax-optimal polynomials (a≈2.68) are too gentle — they equalize perfectly but inflate too slowly. The bifurcation family's structural constraint on coefficient magnitude is a feature, not a limitation. Confirms: you need aggressive equalization (large coefficients) + oscillation cancellation (blending), not gentle equalization (small coefficients, no oscillation).
